Performance Graphs, the Impact of Gamification on Performance

Performance Graphs, the Impact of Gamification on Performance

Gamification is the use of game design elements in non-game contexts to encourage desired types of behaviors (Deterding et al., 2011). Through the process of enhancing services with (motivational) affordances in order to invoke gameful experiences and further behavioral outcomes (Affordances-Psychological Outcomes-Behavioral Outcomes) (Hamari et al., 2014), In recent years, gamification has been applied in many areas, including marketing, politics, health, and education. Current research on gamification has focused on the motivational effects of gamification and on goal-setting-related game elements such as leaderboards, badges, etc. (Sailer et al., 2017; Landers et al., 2017; Hamari, 2017), as goal setting is a common method for enhancing performance in organizations (Locke & Latham, 2002). However, few studies have examined the motivational effects of performance graphs, a feedback-related, game-like element that provides information about the player’s performance compared to their preceding performance during a game, encouraging self-set goals (Sailer et al., 2014). In contrast to leaderboards, performance graphs do not compare the player’s performance to other players, but instead, evaluate the player’s own performance over time. Unlike the social reference standard of leaderboards, performance graphs are based on an individual reference standard. By displaying the player’s performance over a fixed period, people can make comparisons based on their own goals and feedback. Although feedback interventions have been studied for decades and are generally effective (Ilies & Judge, 2005 and Goomas et al., 2011), Kluger & DeNisi (1996) indicate that feedback recipients may be motivated to focus their attention on task motivation or on meta-task processes involving the self, and only individual-aware differences between feedback and goals will have an impact on performance, few empirical studies have been conducted to explore the use of gamification for such interventions.

Hypothesis 1. Participants will perform differently based on the instructions they receive from the performance graph. Specifically, H1a. Quality performance will be higher for participants seeing a performance graph providing similarity feedback than participants seeing no performance graph. H1b. Quantity performance will be higher for participants seeing a performance graph providing quantity feedback than participants seeing no performance graph.

Hypothesis 2. Participants will perform better with the combination of both feedback than would be expected from the main effects alone. Specifically, H2a. Both feedback combined has a significantly larger effect on quality performance as compared to the sum of only similarity feedback and only quantity feedback alone. H2b. Both feedback combined has a significantly larger effect on quantity performance as compared to the sum of only similarity feedback and only quantity feedback alone.

Method

This study will comprise an online randomized between-subjects behavioral experiment involving a brainstorming activity, with additional data collection via an online questionnaire. The experimental task of the study will be a 10-minute brainstorming session in which participants are asked to list the uses of a box, chosen because brainstorming tasks have long been considered ideal for goal-setting studies related to task performance (Litchfield, 2008; Jung et al., 2005; Hinsz, 2005; Landers et al., 2017; White et al., 1995). Participants will be divided into four groups, (1) no performance graph, (2) performance graph provides similarity feedback, (3) performance graph provides quantity feedback, and (4) performance graph provides both similarity feedback and quantity feedback to conduct 2x2 between-subjects experiment. A prototype of the necessary scoring software, in which the website compares each input of the participant and uses natural language processing methods (Cer et al., 2018) to compare the similarity of each input, has already been developed (https://performance-graph-survey.herokuapp.com). This will enable the website’s performance graph to display similarity feedback and quantity feedback in real time. Caliskan et al. (2017) concluded that sentence embeddings can generally be used to reproduce human performance on implicit association tasks for both benign and potentially undesirable associations. To verify that this occurs in the present experiment, a pilot test will be conducted in which participants will be asked to complete the same brainstorming task as will be used in the main study, asking two researchers to count unique ideas from each respondent, and examining convergence in counts between human raters and the algorithmic system.

Research Plan

The research will be divided into the following stages. 1) In the Pilot Study Data Collection stage, N=50 will be collected via the Prolific online panel research service, expected to be completed before Sep 20. 2) In the Pilot Study Analysis stage, pilot data will be checked to verify the scoring model, expected before Sep 30. 3) In the Full Study Data Collection stage, N=200 will be collected via Prolific, expected to be completed before Oct 20. 4) In the Full Study Data Analysis stage, hypotheses will be tested statistically, expected by Oct 30. In the Writing stage, the final paper will be written, with the goal of submission to a journal, by Dec 10. Each experiment is expected to take 10 minutes for each participant, and $2 compensation will be provided (at an equivalent rate of $12/hour). Including fees, costs are expected to equal $2.67 per person, with a total cost of $667.5.

Expected Outcomes

The results of the study will fill a research gap on gamification and provide guidelines for organizations that want to use performance graph game elements. I plan to participate in the Undergraduate Research Symposium to present my result, submit the paper to Minnesota Undergraduate Research & Academic Journal, and open the code of the website used by the experiment on GitHub to facilitate follow-up research.
